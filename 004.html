By adopting this identity, I build models for initiatives that close social and economical disparities within other Indigenous communities.

Reflect on Visits

Throughout this week, my objective is to go from aspiring designer to cultivating the ability to articulate my personal professional identity and vision.

Markel Cormen's overall attitude was seeing the world as one that is in transition. His attitude on design is that is a culture and a practice with the primal matter of transformation. What was most important to me about the presentation was his attitude towards interdisciplinary work, focus on engaging constituencies, and expressing gratitude towards broader collaborations. I was interested in the ways in which Markel works with organizations to align goals with the needs of constituents. I was further interested in the field of speculative design and how Markel applied it to his work. My favorite part was in how Markel integrated performance art into his practice. I was very interested in the field of transition design after Markel's presentation and look forward to diving deeper in research.

Saul Breza's attitude focused on playfulness and humility. I admit to having difficulties during this lecture and the subsequent exercise in which Saul asked us to envision our own deaths. During this lecture, we were awaiting the results of the 2020 Presidential Election in the United States. Much of the work that I engaged in since the beginning of the pandemic centered responses to life-threatening challenges to Black and Indigenous communities, such as the COVID-19 pandemic and police violence within the United States. What I did appreciate most about Saul's presentation was his focus on entrepreneurship and storytelling. It built on the performance art work of Markel while helping me explore ways to address disparities in economic development opportunities. Furthermore, I appreciated Saul's initiatives to address data disparities that affect children. I'll return to Saul's projects to improve my own initiatives on data disparities that impact Indigenous communities.

Sara de Ubieta's attitude conveyed an open-mindedness which centers lifelong learning, trusting intuition, and entering projects with the assumption that I can do anything. The opportunities for DIY biomaterials research and group activities continued in a shared cooking experiment. What was most interesting to me was how biomaterials design, production, and research can be applied to elevate traditional ecological knowledge of Indigenous communities. Further, there is an opportunity to personalize relationships with ecosystems through crafting.
